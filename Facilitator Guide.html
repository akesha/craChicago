Instructional Reflection · internal document

What it is, and how to run a faculty member through it.

A one-page summary of the tool, followed by a facilitator's guide for sitting next to a colleague — or a room of them — and helping them turn the term they just taught into signal they can use in July.

Part one · Summary

The tool, in plain terms.

Instructional Reflection is an end-of-term protocol that helps an instructor in engineering, computer science, data science, informatics, or library science capture specific, evidence-anchored observations from the term they just taught — and turn them into a document they will actually re-open in July, when they have to make decisions about fall.

Without structured capture, you have no signal from this term. You only have mood.

It is built around one premise: faculty teaching tools fail when they sound like productivity tools. So the surface is quiet — cream paper, serif body, no streaks, no dashboards. The work is intellectual, not gamified. Each course gets twenty minutes, one document, and five prompts. Saves locally; nothing leaves the browser.

The protocol

Six steps · ~20 min per course
  1. Canvas data review — before writing. Look at submission patterns, score distributions, engagement timing. Disconfirm memory.
  2. What worked, in concrete terms. Name the incident — the lab, the studio crit, the search log — not the semester.
  3. What broke, in concrete terms. Where students struggled in ways you did not predict.
  4. What surprised you. The data point you did not have a slot in your model for.
  5. One question this term left you with. Not ten. One. Ends with a question mark.
  6. One decision for next year. Reflection that does not produce a change is journaling. Closes the Korthagen loop.

The five qualities it scores for

Each prompt's quality readout, and the July artifact, score against the same five bars — drawn from Schön, Brookfield, and Korthagen:

  • Specific
    Names an incident, not a semester.
  • Multi-lens
    Draws on more than your own view.
  • Evidence-grounded
    References actual artifacts.
  • Asks why
    Moves past description to design.
  • Closes the loop
    Ends in a change you'd act on.

What sits around the reflection

The shelf surfaces four surrounding practices — small artifacts that, accumulated across six years, become a stronger dossier than any single dossier-week effort.

Annual · per academic year

  • Response-to-feedback memo — what was raised, what you're taking up, what you're not, and why.
  • Philosophy addendum — where your thinking shifted, where it was reinforced, what you'd defend more strongly now.

Per cycle · or across terms

  • Retrospective — every five terms: stop, commit, improving without you noticing.
  • Trajectory — a data view of changes attempted and artifacts archived across terms.

The optional SoTL layer

Each course has a Study tab — off by default. When toggled on for a course where the instructor is running an intervention they'd want to defend, it adds a methodological scaffold around the reflection: guiding question, study design, IRB posture, consent approach, target output venue, a mid-term falsifier named in advance, and an end-of-term claim with evidence, generalizability bounds, and the falsifier that would force a retraction.

Reflection ends in a decision. Scholarship ends in a claim. The Study tab is what turns the former into the latter — for one or two courses a year, not all of them.

Privacy posture

Everything saves to the instructor's browser (localStorage). No account, no server. If embedded in Canvas, it is added as an External URL — nothing leaves the device. The facilitator should say this out loud at the top of any session.

Part two · Facilitator guide

How to run someone through it.

The tool implements the protocol; the writing is the instructor's. Your job, as facilitator, is not to write with them — it's to keep them on signal and out of mood, and to make sure they leave with a document they'll re-open in July.

A · Who this is for, and who it isn't.

Built for instructors in engineering, computer science, data science, informatics, and library science. The prompt examples reference studio crits, autograders, leakage in ML projects, search logs, relevance judgments, and Boolean evaluation — that specificity is the cue that the tool was made for these faculty.

It will work for humanities or social-science faculty, but the examples will feel borrowed. If you're facilitating outside the target disciplines, say so up front and tell them to translate.

B · Before the session.

Ask the instructor to arrive with

  • Their Canvas (or LMS) gradebook open in another tab — for Section 00.
  • Any mid-semester surveys, end-of-term evaluations, or TA debrief notes.
  • For CS / data science instructors: autograder failure traces, commit-message patterns, notebook revision histories — even just a feel for where the autograder kept rewarding the wrong thing.
  • For information / library faculty: search-log audits, the reference-desk question log if there is one.
  • For engineering instructors: studio-crit transcripts or photos, design-review notes.
  • One course in mind. Just one to start.

Open the tool yourself first

  • From the Tweaks panel, Load sample courses so you can demo the shelf without entering anyone's real courses.
  • Confirm the term picker reads the right term.
  • Confirm the timer, examples, and concreteness check are on. Toggle the AI second-read on if the cohort is comfortable with it; off if it would be a distraction.
  • Reset (Tweaks → Wipe local data) before handing the device or screen to the instructor.
If you're facilitating a group

One device per instructor. Everything is local-only — there is no shared session. Your role becomes a metronome: keep the room paced, surface the prompts on a shared screen, and read out the coaching language. The writing is silent.

D · Walking through one course.

00

Section 00 — the data review.

~8 minutes · before any writing

This is the most-skipped step and the most important. The point is to disconfirm memory before they start writing about what they think happened. Have them open the LMS and look at three specific things:

  • Submission patterns — which assignments had a long tail of late or missing work? Where did the cohort cluster on time?
  • Score distributions — where is the bimodal? Where is the ceiling effect? Where did everyone get the same grade for different reasons?
  • Engagement timing — discussion-board posts, lab attendance, office-hour logs, page-views on key resources. When did the cohort show up and when did they vanish?

Don't write anything yet. Just look. The point is to find at least one thing that contradicts what you'd have said if I'd asked you in the hallway.

They must tick the "I've reviewed and acknowledge what the data is and isn't" caveat before the tool advances. Don't rush them past it. If they're stuck, the three checkboxes are a fallback — even ticking those three is more than most reflection does.

Watch for

The instructor opens the LMS and immediately starts writing in the prompts. Stop them. The data review is a looking step, not a writing step.

01–05

The five prompts.

~4 minutes each · twenty minutes total

Each prompt has the same structure: a title, an italic instruction, a useful vs. useless example pair (collapsed by default — tell them to open it if they get stuck), a textarea, and below it, three follow-ups that appear after 15 words: lens chips, an evidence slot, and a one-line "why" nudge.

Start typing. Don't aim for the right answer. Aim for the specific one. The lens chips and the evidence slot will show up below — leave them for now. You'll get to them.

The concreteness check will flag hedge phrases — didn't land · seemed engaged · went well · overall · vibe — in a side panel. Tell them up front:

Those flags aren't gates. They're a mirror. If you wrote "didn't land," the tool will ask you what specifically fell short. You can ignore it. But the question is usually worth answering.

06

Lenses and evidence — after each answer.

~30 seconds per prompt · do not skip

Once an answer is past 15 words, four lens chips appear: Your own · Students' · A colleague's · Literature. Hover gives a definition and a concrete example for each. The default for most faculty is to tag only their own, which is also the default in unstructured reflection — and the reason it stays thin.

Click only the lenses you actually drew on for this answer. Don't aspire. If you only used your own view, leave it at one chip. If a TA mentioned something that shaped what you wrote, tag the colleague lens. Even one beyond your own sharpens the read.

The evidence slot is collapsed by default. Ask them to expand it and write a one-line pointer — "week 7 studio crit photos in the shared drive", "DATA 220 final-project leaderboard CSV", "LIS 510 mid-semester survey, question 3". The point isn't a citation. It's that future-them, reading in July, knows where to go look.

07

Closing the loop — Prompt 5.

~5 minutes · this is the one that matters

Prompts 1–4 produce signal. Prompt 5 is what makes the document worth re-opening in July. Don't let them write a list. The prompt asks for one decision — specific enough that future-them could explain it to a colleague before September.

Read your answer to question 4 again. Now: what is the one change you'd make to this course next year because of that question? Not three. One. Specific enough that you'd know exactly what to change.

Watch for

The instructor writes "rework the final project" or "be clearer about LLM policies in the syllabus." Both are aspirations, not decisions. Ask: What would the new final project look like? What sentence would be in the syllabus? The answer they give out loud — write that down.

08

The artifact view.

~5 minutes · first read-through

On close-out the tool opens the July artifact: their five answers stitched into a single document, with the five-quality scoring panel at the bottom. This is the first time they see the term as a single readable piece.

Have them read it through, out loud if they're comfortable. Look for the gaps — a prompt with no lenses tagged, an answer with no evidence pointer, a "decision" that reads like an aspiration. The scoring panel makes these visible without you having to point at them.

Print it, or save the page as a PDF, and put it where you'll find it. Name it after the course and the term. Calendar a re-read for the second week of July.

E · Coaching the prompts.

The single most useful intervention a facilitator can make is to ask "what specifically?" at the right moments. The prompts are written to invite specificity; the work is keeping the writer on it.

On "What worked"

Don't tell me the course went well. Tell me the assignment, the moment, or the question a student asked. Which design choice in the assignment produced that response?

On "What broke"

Tell me the thing you would not have predicted in week one. Where did the autograder reward the wrong thing? Where did the assignment stop measuring what you thought it measured?

On "What surprised you"

What does this surprise contradict in your prior model of how the course works? If nothing contradicts, it isn't a surprise — it's a confirmation. Try again.

On "One question"

One sentence. Ends with a question mark. Not the obvious question. The one you'd still be carrying at the end of August.

On "One decision"

What would have to be true in week one of next term for this change to actually take? If you can't answer that, the decision isn't specific enough yet.

F · Common pitfalls — and how to name them.

Pitfall · Mood, not signal

"It was a tough semester." / "The energy was off." / "Students just weren't engaged." Treat as a tell. Ask: name one moment. What did they do, not how it felt.

Pitfall · The semester, not the incident

"Overall, lab sections went well." Ask: which lab, which week, which question someone asked? The unit of reflection is the incident.

Pitfall · The aspiration, not the decision

"I want to be clearer about AI policy." Ask: what sentence will be in the syllabus on day one?

Pitfall · One-lens reflection

All five prompts tagged with only "Your own." Ask: did any student question, TA comment, or peer observation actually shape what you wrote here? If yes, tag it. If no, leave it — and notice that pattern.

Pitfall · The list-of-ten question

Prompt 4 comes back with six questions. Ask: which one would you still be carrying at the end of August if you wrote down none of them? Cut the rest.

Pitfall · The artifact as a performance

The instructor writes for an imagined dean or P&T committee. The document gets stiff, and the signal disappears. Remind them: the audience is future-you, in July, with a calendar to plan. Nobody else.

G · After the session.

Immediately

  • Confirm the artifact saved (the topbar pill should read saved).
  • Have them print to PDF and stash a copy somewhere outside the browser — Drive, Box, a folder. The localStorage copy is convenient, not durable.
  • Calendar a re-read for the second week of July, before they touch fall syllabi.

Across the term cycle

  • When they make a change they wrote down in Prompt 5, have them log it in What I tried (the workspace tab) — with the change, the why, and eventually the outcome.
  • When they create or revise an artifact (rubric, assignment brief, lab manual), archive it in Artifact archive with a one-line annotation. Future-them, building a dossier, will thank present-them.

Once a year, in May or June

  • Response-to-feedback memo — 30 minutes. Four fields. What was raised in evaluations, what you're taking up, what you're not, and the reasoning. The reasoning is the field that earns credibility; do not skip it.
  • Philosophy addendum — 30 minutes. Three fields. Where your thinking shifted, where it was reinforced, what you'd defend more strongly now. Stacks year-on-year for dossier assembly.

Every five terms

  • Retrospective — an hour. Three fields. What to stop. What to commit to. Where you're improving without noticing. The tool surfaces all the prior-term "decision" answers above the form so you can read them as a body.

When building a dossier

  • Trajectory — not an entry surface. A data view across terms: reflection completion, changes attempted, artifacts archived. Walks the reviewer through the accumulation.

H · When to mention SoTL.

Don't introduce the Study tab in a first session. Most instructors will never need it for most courses, and surfacing it too early dilutes the reflection-ends-in-a-decision frame that makes the core tool work.

Bring it up only when:

  • The instructor describes an actual intervention they're running — "I'm trying a new feedback protocol in DATA 220 this fall", "I redesigned the search-strategy unit and I want to know if it travels."
  • The instructor has a venue in mind — a workshop, a CSEd paper, a teaching-and-learning showcase, an ACRL session, an internal teaching innovation grant.
  • They are willing to name a falsifier before week one. This is the diagnostic question. If the answer is no, the Study tab will produce a worse artifact than the regular reflection.

If you want this course's reflection to travel as scholarship — if you could see yourself writing it up — turn the Study tab on for this course only. The protocol gets a methodological scaffold around it: a guiding question written before week one, a study design, an IRB posture, a mid-term falsifier, and at the end, a claim with evidence and bounds. Otherwise, leave it off. The reflection works as a complete tool without it.

Where to send them

If a SoTL claim is the goal, point the instructor at their unit's teaching-and-learning center for IRB guidance early — not at the end. The single most common SoTL failure mode is "I started collecting data and then realized I needed IRB."
