The one idea

A teaching portfolio is an argument, not an archive.

You are not assembling a scrapbook of everything you have ever taught. You are making a case: here is the kind of teacher I am, here is the evidence, and here is why my choices produced it.

The core of every section
ClaimI am an effective teacher.
EvidenceHere is what my students do, and how they rate the experience.
ReasoningBecause I make intentional, research-informed choices — the part committees almost never get, and the part that makes your case.

The questions you actually get stuck on

Four sticking points for teaching-track CS faculty. The examples are illustrative — written in first person to show the move, not real data.

1 · Student ratings are biased and still weigh heavily

Ratings correlate with instructor gender, race, and accent, and with course difficulty and required-vs-elective status — somewhat apart from teaching quality. Never lead with a raw average. Show context and the trend; pair it with evidence that doesn't depend on perception: concept-inventory gains, DFW rates, artifacts, peer observation. For comments: 4–5 themes, one honest growth area, 2–3 quotes each.

Example. My CS2 ratings averaged 3.9 against a 4.3 department average. Instead of burying it, I noted CS2 is a required, proof-heavy course in a historically low band, showed scores climbing 3.5 → 3.9 → 4.1 over three years, and set them beside a concept-inventory gain and a peer-observation letter. The argument lands: students find this hard, and they're learning more in it than before.
Gather better evidence than end-of-term ratings: early-term feedback (weeks 3–5), a midterm minute paper, a Small Group Instructional Diagnosis (SGID), and peer/consultant observation. Match the instrument to how you teach (lecture, project, lab, distance).
2 · Showing your teaching at scale

Large courses hide individual teaching. Make the systems the evidence: the autograder, the TA training you run, the scalable feedback or mastery scheme, the rubrics that keep many sections consistent. Scalable outcomes: pass/DFW rates, equity-gap movement, retention into the next course.

Example. I coordinate a 320-student CS1 across six sections and nine TAs. Rather than claim one-on-one attention I can't document, I show the autograder I built, the weekly TA calibration and shared rubric, and a DFW rate that fell from 28% to 17% after I added resubmission. The system is the teaching — and that's the evidence.
3 · Team-taught and coordinated courses

State your specific contribution plainly and back it with a co-instructor or course-coordinator letter. Committees cannot infer what you did.

Example. Our software engineering course is team-taught with two colleagues. My portfolio says plainly: I designed and taught the testing and CI/CD modules in weeks 6–9, built the autograded project pipeline, and wrote the rubric the whole team now uses. A letter from the coordinator confirms it, so no one has to guess which parts were mine.
4 · Does CS education work count as scholarship?

Often yes — or it strengthens the case under teaching. Name it: SIGCSE / ITiCSE / ICER papers and posters, published assignment sets or OER, a tool others adopt, curriculum redesign, pedagogical grants. Map each item to the exact category your guidelines use (teaching, scholarship, or service). Teaching-track scholarship usually weighs toward Boyer's Integration and Application, with some Discovery.

Example. I rebuilt data structures around peer instruction and wrote it up as an experience report accepted at SIGCSE. I list it under scholarship with the full citation, note the released question bank as an OER, and name the internal teaching-innovation grant that funded the redesign. Same work, filed in the category my guidelines actually recognize.

Ten strategies for the annual dossier

Condensed from Burnham, Hooper & Wright. These reinforce the archive habit and the 90-day plan.

01Begin on day one. Start an evidence file now; keep everything early, cut later.
02Read your college and university guidelines carefully.
03Listen to senior faculty; they often sit on the review committees.
04Follow the rules for what to include and what to leave out.
05Work consistently through the year, not in a last-minute rush.
06Seek multiple mentors, including recently promoted peers.
07Be transparent. Don't hide weak spots or embellish; both get noticed.
08Keep it consistent and accurate; your CV and dossier must align.
09Address the reviewers' prior recommendations explicitly in your narrative.
10Consult at every stage: before, during, and after.

Turn on the Live Wall — one-time setup (~10 min, free)

The Live Wall uses Google Firebase (free Spark plan). You set up a project once, paste six values into this file, and host it. Until then, the warm-up still saves on each person's own device.

1
Go to console.firebase.google.com and create a project (any name; you can disable Analytics). The free Spark plan is plenty.
2
In the project, click the Web icon (</>) to register a web app. Copy the firebaseConfig object it shows you.
3
Open this HTML file in a text editor, find the LIVE WARM-UP · Firebase setup block near the top, and paste your six values (apiKey, authDomain, projectId, storageBucket, messagingSenderId, appId) between the quotes.
4
In the console, open Build → Firestore Database → Create database. Choose Start in test mode (fine for a workshop), then enable it.
5
Host the file (GitHub Pages, Netlify, or Vercel) and open it. This badge turns green — project this tab and you're live.
