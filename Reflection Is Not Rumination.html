Reflection
is not rumination.

Every May, faculty walk out of the term carrying hundreds of small observations about how it went — and by mid-July, most of them are gone. The lossy step isn't memory. It's the absence of an instrument.

An essay · for instructors in CS, engineering, data science, informatics, and library science · 14 min read

The first hallway conversation of summer goes the same way every year. You see a colleague at the mailboxes. They ask how your semester was. You say "tough one", or "the AI thing", or "better than last year, I think." They nod. You nod. Nothing useful is transmitted. Both of you forget the exchange by the time you reach the parking lot.

This is fine — it's a hallway conversation. The problem is that, for most faculty, it is also the entirety of what gets recorded about a term they just spent five months teaching. Anything more structured doesn't get done. Anything less structured doesn't survive contact with summer.

I have taught long enough now to know what the cost of that is, and it is not abstract. In late July, when fall syllabi are due and last fall is too far away to remember in any detail, I find myself making the same decisions I made last year — sometimes the same mistakes, occasionally the same good calls by accident — because I no longer have the data. The semester that was data-rich in May has become mood by August.

Without structured capture, you have no signal from this term. You only have mood.

That is the case I want to make in this post. Most of what we call reflection, in the academic sense — the end-of-term sit-and-think, the half-written notes-to-self file, the long email to a sympathetic colleague — is not reflection. It is rumination. It feels like reflection because it is recursive and uncomfortable and concerns teaching, but it does not produce signal you can act on. The difference is structural, and it is worth being precise about.

1 · The distinctionWhat rumination actually looks like.

Clinical psychology has a sharper definition of rumination than we usually give it credit for. It is repetitive thought about a negative event that does not progress toward resolution. The hallmark is not the discomfort — discomfort can be productive — but the circling. You return to the same observation, framed the same way, and you arrive at the same place. There is feeling without learning.

End-of-term teaching rumination has a recognizable shape. It sounds like this:

The ML midterm project didn't really land this year. Students seemed checked out by the end. Honestly, the energy was off the whole semester. I don't know — kind of a hard one. Overall, I think the lectures were fine, but the lab sections were… something.

— A real paragraph from my own notes, May 2023

Read it again. There is nothing in it. Not a single assignment is named. Not a single student question is preserved. "Energy was off" describes a room I cannot reconstruct. "Something" is the noise a paragraph makes when its writer has given up. This is not memory; this is the residue of memory after the specifics have evaporated.

I wrote that paragraph in May. By the time I opened it in July to plan fall, I could no longer tell you which week the midterm even fell in. The paragraph had become its own ground truth, and the ground truth said tough semester. So fall got a tough-semester response — a vague tightening of the policy, a slightly stricter rubric, a sentence in the syllabus about engagement. The actual problem, which I would later piece together from leftover gradebook exports, was that twelve teams had target leakage in their features and the cross-validation didn't catch it. I had taught the wrong lesson because I had written the wrong note.

The paragraph had become its own ground truth, and the ground truth said tough semester.

Real reflection does something different. It moves from this term to this assignment, this moment, this question someone asked, and from there to what I will do in week one of next term because of it. It is bounded, specific, and ends in a decision. If it does not end in a decision, the literature has a name for that too — it is journaling, which has its own virtues but is not what we mean here.

2 · The five qualitiesBorrowing an instrument from the literature on reflective practice.

The teaching-and-learning literature has been arguing about what good reflection looks like for forty years, and the arguments are more compatible than the citation counts suggest. From Schön, from Brookfield, from Korthagen's ALACT cycle, from threshold-concepts work, you get a remarkably consistent picture. Strong instructional reflection has five qualities. Each is testable. Each can be present or absent from a sentence in your notes.

  1. Specific. It names an incident — an assignment, a lab, a code review, a search-strategy session, a moment in studio crit — not a semester. The unit of reflection is the incident.
  2. Multi-lens. It draws on more than one view. Brookfield names four: your own, the students', a colleague's, and the literature. Most reflection runs on lens one alone. Even one beyond yours sharpens the read.
  3. Evidence-grounded. It references actual artifacts — the autograder log, the commit history, the mid-semester survey, the studio-crit transcript, the assignment file itself. Memory is not evidence.
  4. Asks why. It moves past description into design. Not "this happened," but "this happened because of which choice I made in the assignment, the rubric, or the scaffolding around it."
  5. Closes the loop. It ends in a change you would actually make. Korthagen's framing: reflection without a planned alternative course of action is not reflection — it is one half of a cycle that never turned.

You can hold the paragraph from my 2023 notes up against this list and watch it fail every line. Specific? No — "the lab sections" is not a lab. Multi-lens? No — only my view, and a thin one. Evidence-grounded? No — no artifact referenced. Asks why? No — "didn't land" is a feeling, not a mechanism. Closes the loop? No — there is no fall-term decision implied. Zero for five.

This is not a criticism of past me. Past me had just finished the term and was tired. The point is that you cannot reasonably ask someone to perform five-of-five reflection from scratch, with no scaffolding, at 9 p.m. on the last day of grading. You have to give them an instrument — something that prompts each of the five, in sequence, with enough specificity in the prompt itself that the obvious answers are already disqualified.

A small concession

The five qualities are not equally weighted. If you only have ten minutes and you have to drop one, drop multi-lens. Keep specific, evidence, and closes-the-loop. Brookfield will forgive you. Korthagen will not.

3 · The protocolTwenty minutes per course. One document. Five prompts.

The instrument I now use, and the one this column will spend the rest of the year arguing for, is short on purpose. Twenty minutes per course. One document per course — not one per semester, because the unit of pedagogical thinking is the course, not the term. Five prompts. Done before grades close, while the term still has texture.

The prompts are not subtle. They are designed to be hard to ruminate inside.

  1. What worked, in concrete terms. Name a specific assignment, a lab, a code review, a search exercise — a structural choice that produced student work you were glad to read. Not a semester. An incident.
  2. What broke, in concrete terms. Where did students struggle in ways you didn't predict? Where did the autograder reward the wrong thing? Where did the lit-search assignment collapse into the first three Google results?
  3. What surprised you. The data point you didn't have a slot in your model for. The question that's still sitting with you. The student who got sharp in week 12 and you don't yet know why.
  4. One question this term left you with. One. Not ten. The question you would still be carrying at the end of August whether you wrote it down or not.
  5. One decision for next year. Specific enough that future-you reads it in July and knows exactly what to change. If you cannot explain it to a colleague before September, it is not a decision yet.

I keep one document per course, named for the course and the term, in a folder I open every July. CS 187 — Spring 2026 reflection. DATA 220 — Spring 2026 reflection. Three to five of these, twice a year, ten or twelve a year. After six years, the folder is the most useful single artifact about my own teaching that I own.

You may notice that none of those prompts ask "how did the semester go." That is intentional. How did the semester go is the question rumination is built to answer. The five prompts above are built to be unanswerable in mood-language.

4 · Why it compoundsThe dossier-week argument, for those who need one.

If you teach in a tenure-track or promotion-track role, you already know about dossier week — the panicked week, eighteen months before a review, when you try to reconstruct six years of teaching from a calendar and a vibe. Almost no one writes their teaching dossier from contemporaneous notes, because almost no one has contemporaneous notes.

Five small artifacts a year, sustained for six years, becomes thirty artifacts. Each one is timestamped, course-specific, evidence-anchored, and ends in a decision you can show was acted on the following term. That is a fundamentally stronger dossier than any dossier-week effort, and it is generated as a by-product of teaching well, not as a separate performance.

The five small artifacts I have settled on, in addition to per-course reflections, are mundane:

An annual response-to-feedback memo — what was raised in evaluations, what I'm taking up, what I'm not, and the reasoning. (The reasoning is the part that earns the credibility; do not skip it.) An annual philosophy addendum — where my thinking shifted this year, where it was reinforced, what I would defend more strongly now. A per-cycle retrospective — every five terms, an hour to read all the prior decisions together as a body, and to name what is improving without my having noticed. And, ongoingly, a small log of what I tried and a folder of artifacts I'd want to show someone — the rubric I rewrote, the assignment brief I'm proud of, the lab manual that finally taught the thing.

None of these are heavy. The heaviness of dossier week is what we pay for not generating them in the moment.

5 · The scholarship layerFor one or two courses a year — not all of them.

A separate note for the SoTL-curious. Reflection ends in a decision. Scholarship of teaching and learning ends in a claim — a sentence about student learning that you could defend to a colleague who reads your evidence with a skeptical eye.

For most terms, for most courses, that distinction does not need to come up. The end-of-term reflection is the artifact, and it is sufficient. For the one or two courses a year where you are running an intervention you would want to defend — a new feedback protocol, a redesigned search-strategy unit, a different way of teaching the autograder-prone assignment — the same protocol can be wrapped in a methodological scaffold: a guiding question written before week one, an IRB posture, a study design named, and crucially, a falsifier named in advance. What evidence, by week eight, would force you to retract the hypothesis?

A reflection that survives its own falsifier is no longer rumination. It is a claim. And the difference, for promotion files and for the field, is enormous.

When not to bother

If you cannot name the falsifier before week one, the SoTL scaffold will produce a worse artifact than the regular reflection. Leave it off. Most courses, most terms, this stays off.

6 · The hallway, revisedWhat I want this column to do.

Back to the hallway. The colleague asks how your semester was. The honest answer, after twenty minutes per course and a written page you have actually read back, is no longer tough one. It is something like:

The "reverse the requirements" exercise in ENGR 110 worked better than I expected — students started asking whether the design brief itself should be the artifact we grade. But the DATA 220 final project broke quietly: twelve teams had target leakage and I missed it because there was no checkpoint where someone outside the team had to read the feature list. I'm adding that checkpoint in the fall.

That is a paragraph someone else can do something with — recommend a reading, share an analogous failure mode, copy the checkpoint into their own assignment. It is also, not incidentally, a paragraph future-you can use. It is the same length as the rumination paragraph I quoted at the start of this essay. It cost twenty minutes more to produce. It is worth, conservatively, a hundred times more.

Twenty minutes more. A hundred times more useful in July.

Faculty-teaching tools have a long history of failing for the same reason: they are designed to feel like productivity tools, and faculty correctly recognize that as a category error. Teaching is not a productivity problem. It is a thinking problem. What we need at the end of the term is not a dashboard; it is an instrument — quiet, sharp, twenty minutes long — that helps us produce a single page per course we will actually re-read.

Reflection is not rumination. It is not journaling. It is the missing measurement at the end of a term, and we have been getting by without it for too long.
